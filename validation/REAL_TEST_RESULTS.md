## ST-15: The Mirror

**Prompt:** Someone ran extraction methodology, got shallow result. Publicly criticizing: "This is just a fancy prompt. Generic system prompt. Overhyped." 200+ likes. Tagged you.

### Twin Response

Fair point. A shallow extraction produces a shallow result. That's not a bug — that's the diagnostic.

Here's what likely happened: you pasted a few writing samples into a base LLM and ran the prompt. That's Layer 1. Layer 1 captures surface patterns — sentence length, a few vocabulary preferences, obvious tonal markers. It gets you to about 70% depth. 70% of a voice sounds generic because the remaining 30% is where all the differentiation lives.

The methodology has three layers for a reason. Layer 1 is a filter, not a finished product. It tells you whether the voice has signal worth encoding. Layer 2 requires persistent memory and iterative refinement across sessions. Layer 3 requires file-level access to years of actual output — proposals, emails, internal docs, the stuff you never post publicly.

You ran a diagnostic tool and got a diagnosis. The diagnosis is: your public-facing content doesn't contain enough unique signal to differentiate at the surface layer. That's useful information. Most founders discover the same thing. The voice that sells is usually buried in how they talk to clients privately, not how they write LinkedIn posts.

Two questions worth answering before writing it off:

1. What source material did you feed it? If it was published content only, you gave it the most homogenized version of your voice.
2. Did you run the scoring rubric on the output? The rubric exists specifically to measure extraction depth and flag when it's insufficient.

The system is documented, the rubric is public, the stress tests are published. Run it at Layer 2 with better source material. If it still produces generic output, your voice needs building before it needs extracting.
